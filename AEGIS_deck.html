The Problem

“Buy Canadian” matters — but nobody can verify it

1.

Security you can't see

Critical electronics — chips, flight controllers, radios — sourced abroad can hide undisclosed parts or firmware-level backdoors. For defence and dual-use systems, an origin you can't verify is a risk you can't manage.

2.

Sovereignty & jobs

Low-cost offshore labour and opaque tiers undercut Canadian industry. Procurement dollars meant to build domestic capability leak abroad when no one can check where value was actually added.

3.

A claim, not a proof

Today “Made in Canada” rests on supplier self-reporting — easy to misrepresent, impossible to audit once a product has passed through many hands.

The Solution

AEGIS — “trust, but verify” for origin

Don't take the supplier's word for it. Submit a product's full attestation chain; AEGIS returns its Canadian-content %, its legal designation, whether the chain is valid, and every anomaly it found — built on the organizer's byte-exact reference crypto.

① Verify every signature

Each contribution is an Ed25519-signed attestation checked against a registered supplier key, and hash-linked to the exact content of the inputs it consumed — a tamper-evident chain of custody.

② Compute the designation

Walk the supply graph, sum direct costs by country of work, and decide Product of Canada / Made in Canada / none against the legal thresholds — deterministically.

③ Detect what self-reporting hides

Catch integrity, replay, quantity, structural and statistical attacks — and stay standing on incomplete data. Stateless, one-command Docker deploy.

POST /verify → { canadian_content_percentage, designation, chain_valid, anomalies[] }

Tamper-Evident

What self-reporting can't catch

Every supplier private key ships in the kit — so an attacker can sign a false chain. A valid signature isn't enough; AEGIS reasons about whether a chain is internally consistent and physically plausible, and classifies each violation.

⮾

Integrity / forgery

Modified after signing, or signed by a key not in the verified registry.

↻

Replay & reuse

One product's attestation dropped into another's chain, or reused across products.

≷

Quantity violations

A chain consumes more material than upstream suppliers ever produced — summed globally across diamonds.

⌥

Structural problems

Missing references, broken hash links, cycles, impossible orderings.

⚑

Statistical outliers

Legal on every rule, yet anomalous vs. how genuine chains look — learned from 1,000 chains.

◌

Incomplete data

Missing fields flag uncertainty — the verifier still returns a useful answer without falling over.
